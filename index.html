Who Am I?

I am Vinukshen Shantharoopan, but to keep life simple, I go by the name Vinu Ruban. I was born in Norway to a Sri Lankan family – my mother tongue is Tamil. I consider myself to be a creative, open-minded and out-going person. My favourite colour is blue – I'm sure you can tell from the design of this website!

Education

After completing my A-Levels in Mathematics, ICT and Art, I couldn't decide on a particular field. So, I studied the course which best widened my knowledge in various fields, such as IT, Finance, Engineering - BSc Mathematics! (which included a placement year with TEFL Trainer in Spain)

Four amazing years of dreaming big and studying smart had me graduating with a First Class Honours!

It was through the intensive courseworks and projects completed at university, involving various programming languages, where I truly realised my passion for coding. •••

Career

I kickstarted my career in Programming by moving to Manchester for 3 months and starting off as a Software Development and Testing Trainee. I completed the challenging training provided by QA Consulting and became specialised in both the development and testing side of software.

The training taught me about a collection of domains, ranging from MySQL and H2 within DBMS, to back-end technologies like Java, to front-end technologies such as JavaScript and HTML. Being a strategic thinker with a logical approach helped me to code accurately and efficiently, as well as to think outside the box while testing the quality of software i.e. its functionality, usability and accessibility.

I have since moved to Brighton to join NETBuilder as an IT Consultant – Software Test Analyst. The first day itself brought great news that we will be working on a project with one of their clients in Jan 2019 – BT!

I will be a Test Analyst at BT in London, on a contract basis. We will be working in groups of 3 where each of us have a major responsibility: Front-end developer, back-end developer, and tester. This is hugely exciting for me as not only is this a prominent company but being the Tester of the group means I can greatly improve my own skills as well as, in a small way, guarantee the satisfaction of BT customers.

Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV)

Final Year Dissertation At Coventry University

Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) is a non-intrusive, optimising method used within both the automotive and aerodynamic industries, to adapt the body of the vehicle such that there is minimal drag force opposing it. The aim is to acquire the local velocity distribution in a flow of air or transparent liquid past an object, by evaluating instantaneous images of the flow structures.

In the duration of my project, I widened my knowledge on the basic theory of PIV and learnt how this technique can be applied to the real world. To begin my exploration on the practical side of this matter, I reviewed the PIV methodology to understand the various approaches, requirements and limitations. Alongside this, I studied and analysed two PIV software and found PIVlab to be most advantageous of the two, in evaluating the images. Subsequently, I carried out a numerical and physical experiment to apply what I had learnt.

In the numerical experiment, I formed a basic code in MATLAB to create artificial PIV-images, and another script to collect data from these images so that I could estimate the resulting velocity field. The existing PIV software which included evaluation techniques such as cross-correlation and Fast Fourier Transform based cross-correlation (FFT-CC), enabled me to develop my own code with the simpler ‘centre of mass’ approach.

In the physical experiment, my supervisor and I built a simple desktop experiment where we imitated the actual PIV setup, which resulted in the desired flow structures being successfully achieved. I had previously reviewed a few papers on these types of experiments, so that I was able to manipulate the most reasonable apparatus, whilst adhering to the other requirements of my investigation. Images of the flow were taken and analysed using PIVlab. By comparing these results to a paper where similar experiments were carried out, I was able to understand how different set-ups, cause differences in results.

  • October 2017 - April 2018
  • Supervisor: Alex Pedcenko
  • Institution: Coventry University

Second year group project

Energy required for the lift in Coventry University's Engineering and Computing building

I worked within a team of four to solve a problem, where we had to estimate the energy required to call the lift in Coventry University's Engineering and Computing building from the second floor to the ground floor and then to transport a 100kg individual to the 3rd floor. Due to my strength in mechanics, I was able to evaluate the theory of this task as it was purely based on 'conservation of energy'. As a team leader, I remained calm and explained the theory and how to execute this problem practically to the entire team. We estimated the height of the building from the lower to the upper ground by counting the number of stairs and multiplying the height of one stair in metres. We managed to estimate the energy from finding the distance of the whole building, the force required for the cable to pull the lift upwards and the force of gravity due to the weight of the lift and the 100kg individual. From this project, I learnt how to explain the method to solve the problem in a good manner and made sure the whole team contributed equally. If I were to complete the project again I would prepare a PowerPoint presentation to explain the problem to my team in depth and also find the blue print of the building to get a more precise value of the height for a better result of the energy required for the lift.

  • January 2016 - May 2016
  • Team: Vinukshen Shantharoopan (Team Leader), Vashini Pulendran & Chirag Naik
  • Institution: Coventry University
