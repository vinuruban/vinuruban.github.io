Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV)

Final Year Dissertation At Coventry University

Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) is a non-intrusive, optimising method used within both the automotive and aerodynamic industries, to adapt the body of the vehicle such that there is minimal drag force opposing it. The aim is to acquire the local velocity distribution in a flow of air or transparent liquid past an object, by evaluating instantaneous images of the flow structures.

In the duration of my project, I widened my knowledge on the basic theory of PIV and learnt how this technique can be applied to the real world. To begin my exploration on the practical side of this matter, I reviewed the PIV methodology to understand the various approaches, requirements and limitations. Alongside this, I studied and analysed two PIV software and found PIVlab to be most advantageous of the two, in evaluating the images. Subsequently, I carried out a numerical and physical experiment to apply what I had learnt.

In the numerical experiment, I formed a basic code in MATLAB to create artificial PIV-images, and another script to collect data from these images so that I could estimate the resulting velocity field. The existing PIV software which included evaluation techniques such as cross-correlation and Fast Fourier Transform based cross-correlation (FFT-CC), enabled me to develop my own code with the simpler ‘centre of mass’ approach.

In the physical experiment, my supervisor and I built a simple desktop experiment where we imitated the actual PIV setup, which resulted in the desired flow structures being successfully achieved. I had previously reviewed a few papers on these types of experiments, so that I was able to manipulate the most reasonable apparatus, whilst adhering to the other requirements of my investigation. Images of the flow were taken and analysed using PIVlab. By comparing these results to a paper where similar experiments were carried out, I was able to understand how different set-ups, cause differences in results.

  • Project Period: September 2017 - April 2018
  • Supervisor: Alex Pedcenko
  • Institution: Coventry University
